The Billable Hours Paradox

BigLaw firms profit by billing more hours. AI makes work faster, reducing billable hours. So why adopt tools that cannibalize your revenue model?

The answer depends entirely on the billing model. Under hourly billing (90% of the market), AI directly destroys firm revenue. Under fixed-fee billing (10% of the market), AI supercharges margins. The transition between these models is glacially slow.

How are profits rising while hours decline?
Pure rate increases. BigLaw billing rates jumped 10% in 2024 (blended to $1,057/hr at AmLaw 100), while hours per lawyer declined 1.6%. Firms are raising prices faster than AI erodes volume. But this is a temporary arbitrage that clients are actively working to close: 64% of in-house counsel expect AI to reduce reliance on outside counsel.

Value Chain Analysis

The right question is not "is legal AI valuable?" (it is). The question is: does value accrue to the middleware layer? Historical precedent says no. Middleware gets squeezed between the platform expanding down and end users building up.

LAYER 1

Model Labs

OpenAI, Anthropic, Google

Capture token/API revenue. Expanding into verticals. Harvey's own CEO says these are "the bigger risk."

Value: High + Growing
LAYER 2

Middleware

Harvey, Legora, CoCounsel

Per-seat SaaS for packaging + workflow. No proprietary model. Moat is security certs and integrations, which are commoditizing.

Value: Uncertain, Likely Declining
LAYER 3

End Users

Law firms, In-house GC, Individuals

Efficiency gains accrue directly. Increasingly using models directly for contract review, redlining, and research.

Value: High + Growing
The middleware precedent
Travel agents were disintermediated by Expedia, then Expedia squeezed by Google Flights. Data analytics dashboards were absorbed into cloud platforms. API management startups were absorbed by AWS/Azure. The wrapper rarely survives the platform's vertical integration.

07

Bottom Line

  1. Harvey and Legora are real businesses with fast-growing revenue. Harvey: ~$210M ARR. Legora: undisclosed but likely $100-170M. The products work and have real customers.
  2. The valuations are not justified by fundamentals. Harvey at $11B is 52x revenue. Normal SaaS trades at 10x. Probability-weighted fair value: ~$2.6B. The remaining $8-9B is a bet on becoming the operating system of law.
  3. The billable hours paradox is real but not the strongest bear case. The stronger argument: middleware squeeze. Harvey sits between model labs (expanding down) and enterprise IT (building up). This layer gets absorbed in platform shifts.
  4. Value accrues to model labs and end users, not middleware. Individuals doing their own legal work with Claude/GPT capture the surplus directly. In-house teams with fixed budgets benefit clearly. The middleware layer's share is the most uncertain.
  5. Revenue growth reflects a land-grab, not proven durability. $50M to $210M in 15 months is impressive. But it's driven by initial enterprise sales cycles and fundraising-as-marketing. Renewal cycles in 2027-2028 are the real test.
  6. Harvey's own CEO says model companies are the bigger threat. When the founder tells you the moat is thin, listen. Harvey uses OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. That alone tells you it's not winning through unique model IP.
  7. VC dynamics explain the valuation better than fundamentals. Sequoia/a16z bidding war. Four valuation jumps in 12 months ($3B, $5B, $8B, $11B). Mega-rounds as procurement signaling to conservative enterprise buyers. Category kingmaking before the window closes.
  8. The right question is not "is legal AI valuable?" It is. The question is whether value accrues to the middleware layer. Historical precedent and structural analysis say: probably not durably. The scaffolding gets absorbed.
